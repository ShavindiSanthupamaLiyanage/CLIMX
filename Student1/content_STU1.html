UNFCCC - The Foundation of Climate Action

The United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC), established in 1992 and in force since March 21, 1994, is the foundation of global climate action. With 198 member countries, it aims to prevent dangerous human interference with the climate system by stabilizing greenhouse gas (GHG) concentrations.

The Convention places primary responsibility on developed nations (Annex I countries) to lead emissions reductions, as they have historically contributed the most to climate change. It also mandates financial and technological support for developing nations, managed through the Global Environment Facility (GEF).

Additionally, the UNFCCC establishes a monitoring and reporting framework, requiring Annex I countries to submit climate policies and annual GHG inventories. Over time, it has evolved to emphasize both mitigation and adaptation, with initiatives like the Cancún Adaptation Framework.

As one of the three Rio Conventions, alongside the Convention on Biological Diversity and the Convention to Combat Desertification, the UNFCCC fosters international cooperation. It paved the way for key climate agreements, including the Kyoto Protocol and the Paris Agreement, making it a cornerstone of global climate governance.

UN Climate Conferences (COPs)

Every year, the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) brings together 198 Parties to discuss and implement measures to combat climate change. The Conference of the Parties (COP) serves as the primary decision-making body under the UNFCCC, where nations negotiate agreements and assess progress toward global climate goals. Since the first COP in Berlin, Germany (1995), these conferences have played a pivotal role in shaping climate policies, leading to crucial treaties such as the Kyoto Protocol (1997) and the Paris Agreement (2015).

Each COP is hosted by a different country, following a rotation system among the five UN regional groups. These conferences have set global milestones by addressing issues like carbon emission reduction, renewable energy transition, and climate adaptation strategies. Below is an overview of five significant COPs and their objectives.

COP29 (2024) – Baku, Azerbaijan

COP29, held in Baku, Azerbaijan, in November 2024, focused heavily on climate finance as a means to accelerate global climate action. The conference concluded with a significant agreement emphasizing the responsibility of developed nations to provide substantial financial assistance to developing countries. The agreement, finalized on 24 November 2024, calls for developed nations to deliver at least $300 billion per year by 2035 to help developing countries mitigate climate change and adapt to its worsening effects.

While the agreement was a crucial step forward, UN Secretary-General António Guterres expressed concerns that it fell short of the level of ambition needed to address the global climate crisis. However, it was recognized as an essential foundation for future negotiations and progress.

COP28 (2023) – Dubai, UAE

COP28, hosted in Dubai, United Arab Emirates, from 30 November to 12 December 2023, was a turning point in the fight against climate change. The conference marked the beginning of the end of the fossil fuel era, with world leaders agreeing on a transition plan that ensures fairness and equity. The meeting also included the first-ever global stocktake of the Paris Agreement, a critical process that assesses countries' progress in reducing emissions.

The outcomes of COP28 included a commitment to deep emission cuts, an accelerated shift towards renewable energy, and a pledge to scale up climate finance. The agreement reinforced the importance of keeping global temperature rise within 1.5°C above pre-industrial levels, as outlined in the Paris Agreement.

Main Objective: Accelerating the transition from fossil fuels and enhancing commitments to limit global warming.

COP27 (2022) – Sharm el-Sheikh, Egypt

Held in Sharm el-Sheikh, Egypt, in November 2022, COP27 focused on moving from pledges to real action. The highlight of the conference was the adoption of the Sharm el-Sheikh Implementation Plan, which laid the groundwork for addressing climate challenges with more accountability. A historic decision was made to establish a dedicated fund for loss and damage, providing financial assistance to vulnerable nations suffering from climate-related disasters.

COP27 also reinforced the need to maintain global warming below 1.5°C and emphasized financial support for developing nations. The conference shifted attention to holding corporations and financial institutions accountable for their climate commitments, ensuring that promises lead to measurable results.

Main Objective: Creating a Loss and Damage Fund and ensuring stronger accountability measures.

COP26 (2021) – Glasgow, UK

COP26, held in Glasgow, United Kingdom, in 2021, was one of the most crucial climate summits, bringing together 120 world leaders and over 40,000 registered participants. The conference resulted in the adoption of the Glasgow Climate Pact, which aimed to strengthen climate resilience, reduce greenhouse gas emissions, and increase financial support for developing nations.

One of the key aspects of COP26 was the renewed focus on climate finance, where developed nations were urged to fulfill their promise of providing $100 billion per year in funding to support developing countries. Additionally, the agreement set a net-zero emissions target and emphasized phasing out coal and fossil fuel subsidies.

Main Objective: Strengthening global climate commitments and advancing net-zero emissions pathways.

Key Climate Agreements

International climate agreements play a crucial role in addressing global environmental challenges by setting legally binding targets and fostering cooperation among nations. Three landmark treaties—the Kyoto Protocol, the Paris Agreement, and the Montreal Protocol—have significantly shaped global efforts to combat climate change.

The Kyoto Protocol introduced the first legally binding commitments for developed nations to reduce greenhouse gas emissions. The Paris Agreement built upon this by uniting countries in a collective effort to limit global temperature rise. Meanwhile, the Montreal Protocol has been instrumental in protecting the ozone layer by phasing out harmful substances. Together, these agreements demonstrate the international community’s commitment to environmental sustainability.

Kyoto Protocol (1997)

The Kyoto Protocol, adopted on December 11, 1997, and enforced on February 16, 2005, was the first legally binding international treaty to reduce greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions. It introduced mandatory reduction targets for developed countries based on the principle of "common but differentiated responsibilities and respective capabilities" (CBDR-RC).

Kyoto Protocol (1997)

The Kyoto Protocol, adopted on December 11, 1997, and enforced on February 16, 2005, was the first legally binding international treaty to reduce greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions. It introduced mandatory reduction targets for developed countries based on the principle of "common but differentiated responsibilities and respective capabilities" (CBDR-RC).

The first commitment period (2008–2012) required 37 industrialized nations and the EU to reduce emissions by 5% below 1990 levels. The Doha Amendment (2012) introduced a second commitment period (2013–2020) with an enhanced reduction target of at least 18% below 1990 levels.

Key mechanisms of the Kyoto Protocol:

  • International Emissions Trading (IET) – Allowing the trading of emission permits.
  • Clean Development Mechanism (CDM) – Supporting green projects in developing nations.
  • Joint Implementation (JI) – Enabling countries to invest in emission reduction projects abroad.

Despite its achievements, the protocol was weakened due to major emitters like the United States refusing to ratify it. It was eventually superseded by the Paris Agreement, which includes all countries in the fight against climate change.

It was the first step towards global climate action.

Paris Agreement (2015)

The Paris Agreement is a landmark international treaty adopted on December 12, 2015, at the UN Climate Change Conference (COP21) in Paris. It represents a global commitment to combat climate change and mitigate its devastating impacts. The agreement's primary goal is to limit global temperature rise to well below 2°C above pre-industrial levels while pursuing efforts to restrict the increase to 1.5°C.

Paris Agreement (2015)

The Paris Agreement is a landmark international treaty adopted on December 12, 2015, at the UN Climate Change Conference (COP21) in Paris. It represents a global commitment to combat climate change and mitigate its devastating impacts. The agreement's primary goal is to limit global temperature rise to well below 2°C above pre-industrial levels while pursuing efforts to restrict the increase to 1.5°C. This is crucial for reducing climate risks such as extreme weather, rising sea levels, and ecosystem disruptions.

The agreement functions on a five-year cycle, where nations submit updated Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs)—outlining their emission reduction plans and adaptation strategies. The first "global stocktake" in 2023, held at COP28, assessed global progress and reinforced commitments toward phasing out fossil fuels in favor of renewable energy.

Recognizing the disparities in resources among countries, the Paris Agreement establishes a financial support system for developing nations to help them mitigate emissions, enhance resilience, and adapt to climate impacts. Developed nations play a key role in funding and technological support. Furthermore, the agreement enforces a transparent framework for monitoring and reporting emissions, ensuring accountability in climate commitments.

The Paris Agreement came into force on November 4, 2016, and has been ratified by 195 Parties (194 states and the European Union), marking a collective global effort to transition towards a net-zero emissions world and achieve the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs).

Montreal Protocol

The Montreal Protocol on Substances that Deplete the Ozone Layer, adopted on September 16, 1987, is a highly successful environmental treaty aimed at phasing out ozone-depleting substances (ODS), such as chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) and hydrochlorofluorocarbons (HCFCs).

Ratified universally by 198 parties, the treaty has implemented a stepwise reduction in ODS production and consumption. The Multilateral Fund, established in 1991, provides financial and technical support to developing countries.

Key amendments:

  • Montreal Amendment (1997) – Accelerated phase-out of HCFCs.
  • Kigali Amendment (2016) – Introduced hydrofluorocarbon (HFC) phase-down, addressing their high global warming potential (GWP).

By phasing out 98% of ODS, the Montreal Protocol has prevented millions of skin cancer cases, helped restore the ozone layer, and significantly contributed to climate mitigation. The treaty is regarded as one of the most successful global environmental agreements.
